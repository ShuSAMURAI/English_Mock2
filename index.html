Passage 1 – The Future of Space Exploration
A
For centuries, humanity has gazed at the night sky and wondered what lies beyond Earth. Early astronomers could only speculate, but the launch of Sputnik in 1957 marked the beginning of a new era: the space age. Since then, milestones such as the Apollo moon landings and the construction of the International Space Station (ISS) have demonstrated our ability to leave Earth and survive in space for extended periods. These achievements, however, are only the first steps in what many scientists view as a long journey toward interplanetary exploration.
B
One of the strongest motivations for space exploration is scientific discovery. Space missions provide data about the origins of the solar system, the formation of planets, and even the possibility of extraterrestrial life. Probes such as Voyager 1 and 2 have transmitted valuable information from the outer planets, while Mars rovers have uncovered evidence that liquid water once existed on the red planet. Each mission brings insights that cannot be gained by Earth-based telescopes alone.
C
Space exploration also has practical benefits for life on Earth. Satellite technology, originally developed for space programs, is now central to weather forecasting, global communication, and navigation systems such as GPS. Materials science has advanced thanks to the unique environment of microgravity, where new alloys and crystals can be tested. Advocates argue that money spent on space missions is not wasted, since innovations developed for astronauts often find important applications in medicine, engineering, and everyday consumer goods.
D
Despite these benefits, critics argue that space exploration consumes enormous resources that might be better spent solving problems on Earth. Launching rockets costs billions of dollars, and missions often experience delays or failures. Some environmentalists also point to the pollution caused by rocket fuel, raising concerns about the sustainability of large-scale exploration. As a result, governments must continually balance public interest in ambitious projects against pressing needs such as healthcare, education, and climate change.
E
In recent years, private companies have begun to transform the landscape of space travel. Firms such as SpaceX and Blue Origin have developed reusable rockets, dramatically lowering launch costs. These companies are not only working in partnership with government agencies like NASA but also pursuing their own commercial goals, such as space tourism and satellite deployment. Some visionaries even suggest that private enterprise will lead the way in colonizing Mars or building permanent settlements on the Moon.
F
Looking further ahead, scientists debate the feasibility of interstellar travel. While current technology allows spacecraft to reach other planets in our solar system, traveling to even the nearest star system, Alpha Centauri, would take tens of thousands of years with conventional propulsion. Researchers are therefore investigating radical new technologies, including nuclear fusion engines and solar sails powered by lasers. Although these concepts remain theoretical, they highlight humanity's determination to push the boundaries of what is possible.
G
Another dimension of space exploration involves international cooperation. The ISS, built and operated jointly by multiple nations, represents one of the most complex engineering projects ever undertaken. Collaboration reduces costs, fosters peaceful relations, and allows countries to share expertise. Future missions to Mars may also require multinational teams, given the enormous expense and technical challenges. Some analysts argue that space exploration could serve as a unifying force for humanity at a time of increasing global division.
H
Ultimately, the future of space exploration will depend on a combination of scientific ambition, technological progress, and societal priorities. Whether humanity chooses to establish colonies on other planets or restrict itself to robotic probes, the drive to explore remains deeply ingrained in our species. The quest for knowledge, survival, and inspiration ensures that space exploration will continue to play a vital role in shaping our destiny.
Passage 2 – The Economics of Mega-Events
For seventeen days every few years, the world is captivated by the spectacle of global competition, whether at the Olympics, the World Cup, or another large-scale sporting festival. Yet once the last athletes and spectators have left, host cities are often left with towering debts and costly maintenance bills. The anticipated boost in tourism and employment rarely offsets the billions spent on construction, security, and administration, leaving taxpayers responsible for years to come.
Bidding for such events is itself a massive undertaking. Candidate cities may spend close to a decade preparing proposals and lobbying international committees. The official process lasts two years, but the hidden costs—feasibility studies, promotional campaigns, and urban land tied up for promised venues—can run into tens of millions. These bids can still fail, as decisions often hinge on private networks and political considerations rather than merit alone.
Once hosting rights are secured, the problem of spiralling expenses begins. Specialized stadiums and arenas are almost always over budget. Montreal took thirty years to pay off debts from the 1976 Olympics, while London's 2012 Games ended up costing several times the original estimate. Economists describe this as "mega-event inflation," a near-inevitable feature of one-off projects that are highly visible and politically sensitive.
Supporters argue that cities benefit from upgraded airports, transport networks, and international exposure. Some, like Barcelona in 1992, successfully integrated Olympic redevelopment into long-term planning, creating a revitalized waterfront that still attracts visitors. Others point to the intangible "feel-good factor" of hosting: a sense of unity and pride that can boost national morale.
But critics highlight the "white elephant" legacy of empty stadiums and underused sports complexes. Even in countries with strong athletic traditions, taxpayers foot the bill for maintaining venues no longer needed at full scale. Greece's abandoned facilities from the 2004 Games became a symbol of waste, while Rio de Janeiro struggled to find uses for its Olympic sites within months of the 2016 closing ceremony.
Inequality is another concern. International committees consistently prefer wealthy global capitals over provincial cities. This ensures that infrastructure improvements are concentrated in places already well served, reinforcing regional divides. Smaller cities and rural populations gain little from the billions spent.
To address these problems, reformers have suggested bold alternatives. Some propose designating a permanent host city purpose-built for recurring Games. Others advocate extending the duration of events so that facilities are used for months instead of weeks, easing congestion and distributing benefits more evenly. Another option is to decentralize competitions across multiple countries, lowering the financial burden on any single host.
Ultimately, the debate reflects a larger question: should mega-events prioritize spectacle at any cost, or should they be reshaped to serve sustainability and fairness? Without reform, the thrill of global celebration may continue to be followed by decades of financial regret.
Passage 3 – Plain English
There is no theoretical limit to the number of special purposes to which language can be put. As society develops new facets, so language is devised to express them. However, the result is often that language becomes very specialised and complex, and complications arise as ordinary people struggle to make sense of it.
Popular anxiety over special uses of language is most markedly seen in the campaigns to promote 'plain' speaking and writing – notably, the Plain English movements of Britain and the USA. The main aim of these campaigns is to attack the use of unnecessarily complicated language ('gobbledegook') by governments, businesses and other authorities whose role puts them in linguistic contact with the general public. The campaigners argue that such language, whether spoken or written, should be replaced by clearer forms of expression.
The movements took shape only in the 1970s, so it is too soon to ascertain their long-term influence on the characteristics of language varieties. But they have certainly played a major part in promoting public awareness of the existence of communication problems, and have influenced many organisations to do something about it. In Britain, the campaign was launched in 1979, by a ritual shredding of government forms in Parliament Square, London. By 1982, the government had published a report telling departments to improve the design of forms, and to abolish those that were unnecessary. By 1985, around 15,700 forms had disappeared and 21,300 had been revised. In the USA, President Carter's Executive Order of March 1978 required regulations to be written in plain English, and although this was revoked by President Reagan in 1981, it promoted a great deal of legislation throughout the country, and an increase in plain English usage amongst corporations and consumers.
Today the Plain English campaigns continue to grow, focusing especially on such everyday consumer literature as forms, official letters, licences, leases, contracts, insurance policies and guarantees. In Britain, annual publicity is given to the Plain English Awards competition, which gives trophies to organisations that have produced the clearest documents, and booby prizes (the Golden Bull Awards) to those whose materials are least intelligible. In the USA, similar interest is shown in the annual Doublespeak Awards, awarded by the National Council of Teachers to 'American public figures who have perpetrated language that is grossly unfactual, deceptive, evasive, euphemistic, confusing or self-contradictory.'
In these cost-conscious days, it is stressed that clear language not only avoids anxiety on the part of the recipient, it also saves time and money. The campaigns have large dossiers of problem cases. In one case, an official government letter provoked so many complaints and questions that a second letter had to be sent to explain the first. In another, an application form was wrongly filled in by 50% of the applicants, which resulted in a considerable outlay of effort in returning and reprocessing the form. In contrast, there are cases of businesses revising their literature to avoid legal jargon, and benefiting from increased sales.
Particular concern is expressed about the ambiguities and omissions found in medical labels. For example, in one pharmaceutical survey, the instruction to 'use sparingly' was misunderstood by 33% of patients. The instruction 'take two tablets four hourly' received a number of interpretations (e.g. to take eight tablets an hour). Related areas of concern include the use of warning labels on household goods (such as disinfectants) and on toys for children.
The instructions accompanying do-it-yourself products are also regularly cited as a source of unnecessary expense or frustration. Few companies seem to test their instructions by having them followed by a first-time user. Often, essential information is omitted, steps in the construction process are taken for granted, and some degree of special knowledge is assumed. This is especially worrying in any fields where failure to follow correct procedures can be dangerous.
Objections to material in plain English have come mainly from the legal profession. Lawyers point to the risk of ambiguity inherent in the use of everyday language for legal or official documents, and draw attention to the need for confidence in legal formulations, which can come only from using language that has been tested in courts over the course of centuries. The campaigners point out that there has been no sudden increase in litigation as a consequence of the increase in plain English materials.
Similarly, professionals in several different fields have defended their use of technical and complex language as being the most precise means of expressing technical or complex ideas. This is undoubtedly true: scientists, doctors, bankers and others need their jargon in order to communicate with each other succinctly and unambiguously. But when it comes to addressing the non-specialist consumer, the campaigners argue, different criteria must apply.
